# Security and Compliance Edge Cases

## Fair Housing Act Compliance

### Discriminatory Language in Listing Descriptions

**Failure Mode**

A property manager submits a listing description containing phrases such as "perfect for young professionals," "ideal for Christian families," "quiet neighborhood — no section 8," or "walking distance to [ethnically specific landmark] — great for [ethnic group] renters." The listing passes basic profanity and length validation but is published without triggering any review. The platform's algorithmic pricing engine, trained on historical transaction data, assigns higher suggested rent to properties in neighborhoods with historically low minority occupancy, amplifying existing segregation patterns through a feedback loop. Individual listing violations fall under 42 U.S.C. § 3604(c), which prohibits any notice, statement, or advertisement that indicates a preference, limitation, or discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, familial status, national origin, or disability.

**Impact**

Published discriminatory listings expose the platform to HUD enforcement actions, private plaintiff lawsuits, and state attorney general investigations under state-level analogs (e.g., California's FEHA, New York Human Rights Law). Disparate-impact pricing, even without discriminatory intent, violates the Fair Housing Act under the Supreme Court's ruling in *Texas Department of Housing & Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc.* (2015). Financial penalties reach $16,000 for a first violation and $65,000 for subsequent violations under 42 U.S.C. § 3612. Reputational harm from a single viral listing can trigger platform-wide advertiser and lender partner exits.

**Detection**

Deploy a multi-stage NLP pipeline before any listing reaches the published state:

1. **Token classification layer**: Fine-tune a BERT-based sequence labeler (e.g., `bert-base-uncased` with a token classification head) on a curated dataset of Fair Housing violations sourced from HUD complaint archives. The model emits per-token probability scores for membership in protected-class categories: `RACE`, `RELIGION`, `NATIONAL_ORIGIN`, `FAMILIAL_STATUS`, `DISABILITY`, `SEX`, `COLOR`.

2. **Phrase-level blocklist**: Maintain a regularly updated blocklist of exact and fuzzy-matched phrases using a trigram similarity index (pg_trgm in PostgreSQL or a Redis-based Bloom filter for sub-millisecond lookup). Flag phrases like "no kids," "adults only," "walking distance to [mosque/temple/church]," "ESL-friendly," and Section 8 refusals.

3. **Disparate-impact audit on pricing model**: Run quarterly Adverse Impact Ratio (AIR) analysis on the rent suggestion model. Compute the ratio of median suggested rent for majority-minority census tracts vs. comparable majority-white tracts, controlling for square footage, bedroom count, amenity score, and walkability index using a ridge regression residual. An AIR below 0.8 triggers a bias remediation cycle per the EEOC's four-fifths rule, adapted to housing pricing. Feed model outputs into a disparate impact dashboard visible to a Fair Housing compliance officer role.

4. **Re-training guard**: Any model retrain job that shifts the AIR below 0.85 on the held-out validation set is blocked by a CI/CD gate. The gate runs before any model artifact is promoted to the serving registry.

**Mitigation**

All listing submissions pass through a synchronous NLP scoring endpoint before the record transitions from `DRAFT` to `PENDING_REVIEW` status. Listings with a protected-class token probability above 0.72 (tunable threshold) are routed to `FLAGGED_COMPLIANCE_REVIEW` and surfaced in a compliance queue. Agents receive an inline explanation ("This phrase may indicate a preference for or against a protected class") and a suggested rewrite generated by a prompt-engineered LLM with a system instruction constrained to neutral, factual property descriptions. The platform prohibits direct submission bypass — the API enforces state machine transitions that reject any attempt to move a listing to `PUBLISHED` while its compliance score flag is active.

Pricing model training pipelines exclude census tract racial composition features and any features with a Pearson correlation above 0.4 with protected-class demographic variables (using a correlation-based feature filter logged in the model card). Remaining features are audited annually by an external Fair Housing expert.

**Recovery**

When a discriminatory listing is discovered post-publication (via user report, HUD complaint, or retroactive NLP scan during a model upgrade), the platform executes an automated `TAKEDOWN` workflow: the listing status transitions to `REMOVED_COMPLIANCE`, all cached CDN copies are purged via cache invalidation API within 60 seconds, and the syndication feed emits a deletion record to all downstream listing aggregators (Zillow, Realtor.com, etc.) within one polling cycle. A compliance incident record is created in the audit log with immutable timestamps (append-only table, `INSERT` only, no `UPDATE`/`DELETE` permissions on the audit schema). The property manager's account is suspended pending review. HUD self-reporting is initiated within 24 hours per best practice to demonstrate good-faith compliance posture.

## GDPR/CCPA Tenant Data Rights

### Right to Erasure vs. Seven-Year Lease Record Retention Conflict

**Failure Mode**

A tenant submits a verified Right to Erasure request under GDPR Article 17 (or a deletion request under CCPA/CPRA, Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.105). The platform's automated erasure workflow cascades a `DELETE` across all tenant-linked tables — `tenants`, `lease_applications`, `payment_history`, `maintenance_requests`, `communication_logs` — before legal holds have been evaluated. This destroys records that the platform is legally required to retain for 7 years under the IRS's guidance on business records (IRC § 6501 statute of limitations period), state landlord-tenant laws mandating retention of security deposit accounting (e.g., California Civil Code § 1950.5(g)), and anti-money-laundering obligations under 31 U.S.C. § 5318 (Bank Secrecy Act). Alternatively, the inverse failure occurs: the platform performs no erasure and retains all tenant data indefinitely, violating data minimization principles and the erasure right.

**Impact**

Premature erasure exposes the platform to civil liability from landlord clients who can no longer produce required financial records in an audit or eviction proceeding. GDPR non-compliance for unlawful refusal to erase carries fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover under GDPR Article 83(5). Failure to honor CCPA deletion rights exposes the platform to California AG enforcement actions and private right of action for data breaches involving undeleted data (§ 1798.150). Over-retention that is discovered in litigation triggers adverse inference instructions to juries.

**Detection**

The erasure request handler must invoke a `LegalHoldEvaluator` service before any data is modified. This service queries:

- **Active lease hold**: Is there an active or recently terminated (< 7 years) lease associated with this tenant profile? Query `leases` table for `tenant_id` with `end_date > NOW() - INTERVAL '7 years'`.
- **Pending litigation flag**: Is the tenant_id referenced in `litigation_holds` table with `status = 'ACTIVE'`?
- **Regulatory minimum**: Is any financial transaction record linked to this tenant within the IRS 7-year window or BSA 5-year window?
- **Pending adverse action**: Is there an open background check adverse action notice period (FCRA-mandated 5 business days) that has not yet expired?

Detection of any active hold must immediately halt the erasure pipeline and return a structured hold reason to the requesting workflow.

**Mitigation**

Implement a **tiered pseudonymization and selective erasure** strategy rather than binary delete/retain:

1. **PII segregation schema**: Store direct identifiers (name, email, phone, SSN, DOB) in a dedicated `tenant_pii` table with a surrogate `pii_token` UUID as the foreign key into all other tables. The main `leases`, `payment_history`, and `maintenance_requests` tables reference only `pii_token`, never raw PII columns.

2. **Erasure = PII table delete + token orphaning**: When an erasure request clears the `LegalHoldEvaluator`, delete or overwrite the `tenant_pii` row. All related records in other tables are now effectively anonymized — they reference a dangling `pii_token` with no resolvable identity. This satisfies GDPR's pseudonymization standard (Recital 26) while preserving financial aggregates for landlord accounting.

3. **Legal hold path**: If the `LegalHoldEvaluator` returns one or more active holds, the system retains `tenant_pii` but applies a `legal_hold_erasure_queued = TRUE` flag and a `scheduled_erasure_date` calculated as the maximum hold expiry date. A scheduled job re-evaluates and executes the erasure automatically when all holds expire. The tenant is notified within 30 days (GDPR Article 12(3)) that erasure has been deferred and the reason (without disclosing litigation-privileged detail).

4. **Consent and purpose ledger**: Maintain a `data_processing_consent` table recording the lawful basis for each data category (contract performance, legal obligation, legitimate interest). Erasure requests are evaluated against these bases — data retained under "legal obligation" cannot be erased; data retained only under "consent" or "contract performance" (where the contract has ended) must be erased on request.

**Recovery**

If erasure was executed prematurely (race condition between the hold evaluator and the delete transaction), initiate a point-in-time restore from the database backup snapshot taken at the last backup window (maximum 24-hour RPO under the backup SLA). Restored records are placed into a quarantine schema with no application read access. Legal and compliance teams manually validate the minimum required record set, re-insert only the legally mandated subset into production, and re-execute erasure on the remainder. A post-mortem documents the race condition root cause and the fix — typically wrapping the hold evaluation and the erasure in a serializable transaction with a `SELECT ... FOR UPDATE` lock on the `tenant_pii` row.

## PCI-DSS Compliance for Rent Payment Processing

### Tokenization, Scope Reduction, and Merchant-of-Record Classification

**Failure Mode**

The platform integrates a payment processor (e.g., Stripe, Braintree) but stores the raw bank account number or card PAN in the application database after the initial transaction — either because a developer logs the full payment object for debugging, a webhook handler persists the raw payload, or a legacy migration copies unmasked ACH account numbers into a reporting table. Even a single column storing a full PAN or unencrypted bank routing+account number pair in a non-CDE (cardholder data environment) table causes scope expansion: the entire application infrastructure is now in PCI-DSS scope, triggering the full SAQ-D self-assessment questionnaire (329 requirements) rather than the SAQ-A (22 requirements) applicable to fully outsourced card processing.

**Impact**

Scope expansion to SAQ-D requires quarterly network vulnerability scans by an Approved Scanning Vendor (ASV), annual penetration testing, file integrity monitoring, and a full QSA audit engagement — adding $50,000–$250,000 annually in compliance costs. A breach of stored PANs triggers mandatory card brand notification (Visa CISP, Mastercard SDP), potential card brand fines of $5,000–$100,000/month, and card-not-present fraud liability shift to the platform. ACH data breaches trigger Nacha Operating Rules violations and potential network suspension. For a proptech platform processing high-volume recurring rent payments, this is an existential risk.

**Detection**

Deploy a continuous data discovery scanner (e.g., a scheduled job using regular expressions conforming to the Luhn algorithm for PANs, and ABA routing number patterns for ACH) across all non-CDE database tables, S3 buckets, log files, and message queue payloads. Run this scan daily during off-peak hours. Flag any match as a Critical finding in the security ticketing system with an SLA of 4 hours to remediation or verified false-positive classification. Log scanning results to an append-only audit table. Additionally, configure Datadog or Splunk APM tracing to alert when any variable named `account_number`, `card_number`, `pan`, `routing_number`, or `cvv` appears in application logs at any log level.

**Mitigation**

**Tokenization vault architecture**: Use the payment processor's tokenization vault exclusively. The platform stores only the processor-issued token (e.g., Stripe's `pm_xxxxx` or Braintree's payment method nonce) in the `payment_methods` table. The token is a randomly generated opaque string with no mathematical relationship to the underlying PAN or ACH details, rendering it useless outside the processor's vault. Never call the processor API in a context where the raw account details pass through platform servers — use client-side JavaScript SDKs (Stripe.js, Braintree.js) or hosted fields iframes to collect card data directly in the processor's iframe, bypassing the platform's servers entirely.

**Scope reduction controls**:
- Network segmentation: CDE components (if any) are isolated in a dedicated VPC subnet with no egress to the general application network except through a WAF-protected reverse proxy. Security groups permit only port 443 from application subnets.
- Webhook payload stripping: Inbound payment processor webhooks are received by a dedicated Lambda function that validates the webhook signature (HMAC-SHA256), extracts only non-PCI fields (transaction ID, status, amount, timestamp, tenant_id), and publishes a sanitized event to an internal SNS topic. Raw webhook payloads are never persisted.
- Log scrubbing middleware: A logging middleware layer intercepts all structured log entries and applies a regex-based scrubber that replaces any matched PAN/ACH/CVV values with `[REDACTED]` before the entry reaches the log sink.

**SAQ-A eligibility maintenance**: Document in the annual PCI-DSS assessment package that all payment page elements are served from the processor's domain (or a processor-hosted iframe), that the platform never receives, transmits, or stores PANs, and that the processor is a PCI-DSS Level 1 certified service provider (evidenced by their Attestation of Compliance on file). This maintains SAQ-A eligibility and limits the platform's assessment scope to 22 controls covering physical security and incident response.

**Merchant-of-record clarification**: In a marketplace model where the platform collects rent on behalf of landlords and disburses to landlord bank accounts, the platform is the merchant of record for all transactions. This means the platform bears full PCI-DSS responsibility, Nacha ACH originator obligations, and money transmission licensing requirements (varies by state — 49 states require a Money Transmitter License for this model; exceptions include agents acting under a licensed money transmitter via a sponsorship arrangement). Ensure the legal entity structure, processor agreement, and state licensing align with the actual money flow.

**Recovery**

Upon discovery of unintended PAN or ACH storage, immediately execute a containment sequence: (1) quarantine the affected table by revoking all application role `SELECT` grants and replacing them with a view that masks the sensitive column (`LEFT(account_number, 4) || '****'`), (2) initiate a forensic investigation to determine the exposure window and whether any unauthorized access occurred (review `pg_stat_activity` history, VPC flow logs, and CloudTrail), (3) notify the acquiring bank and card brands within the timeframe specified in the processor agreement (typically 24–72 hours for a confirmed breach), (4) engage a PCI Forensic Investigator (PFI) as required by card brand rules when a breach is confirmed, (5) purge the raw data using a cryptographic erasure technique (delete the encryption key for encrypted columns, overwrite with zeros for unencrypted columns, then `VACUUM` in PostgreSQL to reclaim pages).

## Property Ownership Fraud Prevention

### Title Fraud, Listing Impersonation, and Wire Fraud in Closing Transactions

**Failure Mode**

A fraudster creates a platform account using a stolen identity, uploads a forged deed or title document for a property they do not own, and publishes a listing offering the property for rent or sale at below-market rates to attract victims. Victims wire a security deposit or down payment to a fraudster-controlled account. In a variant attack, a fraudster intercepts a legitimate closing transaction via a business email compromise (BEC) attack on the title company or real estate attorney, substituting fraudulent wire instructions for the legitimate escrow account. The FBI IC3 2023 report identified real estate wire fraud as a $446 million annual loss category. Title fraud (also called deed fraud or home equity theft) is enabled when county recorder offices accept documents without robust identity verification.

**Impact**

Victims lose irreversible wire transfers (ACH and SWIFT wires are final upon settlement). The platform faces civil liability for negligent enablement of fraud if it failed to implement reasonable identity verification on listing creation. Regulatory exposure includes FTC Act Section 5 (unfair or deceptive acts), state consumer protection statutes, and potential FinCEN scrutiny if the platform processed payments in the fraud transaction. Reputational damage is severe — a single high-profile wire fraud case results in media coverage that quantifiably reduces new user registrations.

**Detection**

**Ownership verification pipeline**:
1. During listing creation, require upload of the current property deed. Run the document through an OCR pipeline (AWS Textract or Google Document AI) to extract the grantor, grantee, legal description, and recording date.
2. Query the county assessor/recorder API (where available — currently accessible for ~60% of U.S. counties via services like ATTOM Data Solutions or First American's PropertyPoint API) to cross-reference the uploaded deed's grantee name against the recorded owner on file. A name mismatch above a configurable fuzzy-match threshold (Jaro-Winkler distance < 0.92) routes the listing to manual review.
3. For counties without API access, require the listing agent/owner to complete a notarized identity attestation and upload a government-issued ID. Perform liveness-checked biometric verification (e.g., Socure, Persona, or Jumio) comparing the ID selfie against the uploaded photo ID.
4. Monitor for impersonation signals: duplicate property addresses across multiple lister accounts, listings created within 48 hours of account creation for high-value properties, and IP geolocation mismatches between the account's historical sessions and the property's state.

**Wire fraud prevention**:
Monitor all outbound payment instruction changes. Any modification to a closing wire instruction (payee account, routing number, bank name) within 72 hours of a scheduled closing triggers a multi-factor re-verification: the instruction change must be confirmed via an out-of-band phone call to a pre-registered phone number and a signed confirmation from the title company's compliance officer using a platform-registered email domain. Display persistent in-app and email warnings on every closing checklist step: "Always verify wire instructions via phone using a number you independently source — never use contact info from an email."

**Mitigation**

Implement a **listing trust score** computed at publication time, incorporating: ownership verification result (deed match, biometric match, notarization), account age, number of prior verified listings, payment history on the platform, and county recorder cross-reference result. Listings below a trust score threshold are displayed with a "Unverified Ownership" badge and are excluded from featured placement. All financial transactions (security deposits, application fees, closing payments) for unverified listings are held in escrow for a 5-business-day fraud review window before disbursement.

Integrate with the National Association of Realtors' fraud alert registry and the American Land Title Association's wire fraud alert service. Subscribe to FinCEN's real estate Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs) to identify high-risk cash transaction patterns in covered metropolitan areas.

**Recovery**

Upon confirmed listing fraud: immediately suspend the fraudster account, freeze any platform-held funds (security deposits in escrow), and submit a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) to FinCEN within 30 days per BSA obligations (31 U.S.C. § 5318(g)) if the platform qualifies as a financial institution or money services business. Coordinate with law enforcement (FBI IC3 complaint, local field office notification) and provide transaction logs, IP addresses, device fingerprints, and document uploads under a lawful subpoena or emergency disclosure request. Notify affected victims within the timeframe required by applicable state data breach notification laws (ranging from 30 to 90 days). For wire fraud victims, immediately contact the receiving bank's fraud department to initiate a SWIFT gpi recall or Fedwire reversal request — success rate is low but time-critical (highest within 24 hours of settlement).

## Background Check Data Handling

### FCRA Compliance for Tenant Screening, Adverse Action Notices, and Data Retention

**Failure Mode**

The platform integrates a consumer reporting agency (CRA) for tenant background checks (credit report, criminal history, eviction history) and returns screening results directly to the landlord without providing the required FCRA disclosures to the applicant. The landlord uses the report to deny the application without sending an adverse action notice. In a second failure mode, the platform retains the background check report data — including credit scores, criminal records, and eviction judgments — in the application database indefinitely, beyond the permissible reporting period, and surfaces this data in future applications or algorithmic scoring without the tenant's knowledge.

**Impact**

Violations of 15 U.S.C. § 1681b (permissible purpose), § 1681d (disclosure requirements for investigative consumer reports), § 1681e(b) (maximum possible accuracy), and § 1681m (adverse action requirements) expose the platform and landlord clients to statutory damages of $100–$1,000 per willful violation, actual damages, punitive damages, and attorney's fees under 15 U.S.C. § 1681n. The CFPB has assessed multi-million dollar penalties against CRAs and platforms that systematically violated FCRA procedures. Class action exposure for tenant-facing violations across thousands of applications is substantial. Additionally, EEOC guidance (and HUD's interpretation for housing) restricts blanket criminal history exclusions that create disparate impact on protected classes.

**Detection**

Instrument the application state machine to enforce FCRA procedural checkpoints as required state transitions:

- `BACKGROUND_CHECK_INITIATED` → requires logging of permissible purpose assertion by landlord (tenancy determination, per § 1681b(a)(3)(F)), tenant consent form completion, and CRA disclosure delivery (Summary of Rights under the FCRA, 16 C.F.R. Part 698 Appendix K).
- `REPORT_DELIVERED_TO_LANDLORD` → blocks the `APPLICATION_DENIED` transition until `ADVERSE_ACTION_NOTICE_SENT` is confirmed (for any denial where the report was a factor).
- `ADVERSE_ACTION_NOTICE_SENT` → requires logging of notice delivery timestamp, delivery method (email/mail), and inclusion of required elements (name and address of CRA, right to dispute, right to free report within 60 days).

Alert if any `APPLICATION_DENIED` event is recorded within 24 hours of `REPORT_DELIVERED_TO_LANDLORD` without an intervening `ADVERSE_ACTION_NOTICE_SENT` event.

**Mitigation**

**Permissible purpose gating**: The CRA API call is only invoked after the applicant has completed a verified consent flow: e-signature on a FCRA disclosure form, explicit consent checkbox (separate from general terms of service per § 1604.5 of Regulation B), and identity verification confirming the consent signatory is the applicant. The consent record is immutable and stored with a cryptographic hash of the form version.

**Adverse action workflow**: When a landlord selects any rejection reason referencing the background report (or any reason in a system where a report was pulled), the platform enforces a mandatory adverse action notice generation step before the rejection is finalized. The notice is generated from a compliant template including all § 1681m(a) required elements and is delivered to the applicant's verified email address with read-receipt logging. Landlords cannot mark the application as denied in the system until the adverse action notice delivery is confirmed.

**Data retention enforcement**: Background check reports (raw CRA responses) are stored in an isolated `screening_reports` table with a `purge_after` timestamp set to 2 years from the report date (a conservative retention limit — the FCRA's § 1681c(a) prohibits CRAs from reporting adverse information beyond 7 years, but platforms retaining raw report data should apply stricter internal limits). A nightly scheduled job (`purge_expired_screening_data`) hard-deletes rows past their `purge_after` date and logs the purge count to the compliance audit table. Report data is never used in any algorithmic scoring model after the application decision is finalized.

**Recovery**

When an adverse action notice violation is identified (e.g., via applicant complaint or internal audit), immediately send a corrective adverse action notice to the affected applicant. Document the violation in the compliance incident log. If the applicant has already been denied housing and lost out on the rental as a result, assess eligibility for remediation (e.g., refund of application fee, priority consideration for a future vacancy). Engage outside FCRA counsel to evaluate class exposure if the violation is systemic. Implement a retroactive audit of the past 24 months of application decisions to identify all cases where adverse action notice delivery is unconfirmed.

## Eviction Record Misuse

### Incorrect Reporting to Credit Bureaus and FCRA Furnisher Obligations

**Failure Mode**

The platform acts as a data furnisher to Equifax, TransUnion, and Experian — reporting eviction judgments, unpaid rent, and lease violations as part of a landlord credit reporting feature. A tenant disputes an eviction record that was reported in error (e.g., the eviction was filed but later dismissed, or the record belongs to a different tenant with a similar name). The platform's dispute handling process ignores the dispute, fails to investigate within the required 30-day window, or re-reports the erroneous record after a brief suppression. In a second failure mode, the platform reports eviction filings (not judgments) as adverse items — eviction filings that were never adjudicated are not reportable consumer information under the FCRA and their reporting constitutes a prohibited practice.

**Impact**

Reporting dismissed evictions, unfiled judgments, or misattributed records violates 15 U.S.C. § 1681s-2(b), which imposes specific dispute-investigation obligations on furnishers. The CFPB's Supervisory Highlights have cited furnishers for inadequate dispute processes and inaccurate tradeline reporting. Statutory damages per violation ($100–$1,000 for willful violations), multiplied across a tenant base of thousands, generate class action exposure in the tens of millions. Inaccurate eviction records have a severe and disproportionate impact on low-income tenants' ability to secure housing, creating fair lending and fair housing liability intersections. TransUnion and Equifax have faced multi-hundred-million dollar settlements for systemic furnisher accuracy failures.

**Detection**

Implement a furnisher data quality validation layer that runs before every Metro 2 formatted data file is transmitted to the credit bureaus:

1. **Status-to-reportability mapping**: Maintain an enumerated mapping of `eviction_case_status` values to reportable/non-reportable flags. Only `JUDGMENT_ENTERED` and `WRIT_OF_POSSESSION_ISSUED` statuses are reportable. `FILED`, `PENDING`, `DISMISSED`, `SETTLED_NO_JUDGMENT`, and `VOLUNTARILY_DISMISSED` are non-reportable. Any attempt to include a non-reportable status in the Metro 2 file triggers a pipeline abort with a compliance alert.
2. **Identity match confidence score**: Before attributing an eviction record to a consumer profile, require a minimum identity match confidence score (name, address, DOB, last-4 SSN) above 0.95 using a weighted scoring algorithm. Records below this threshold are quarantined for manual review rather than furnished.
3. **Dispute tracking SLA monitor**: A Datadog or PagerDuty alert fires when any dispute record in `furnisher_disputes` table has `status = 'OPEN'` and `created_at < NOW() - INTERVAL '25 days'` — providing a 5-day buffer before the 30-day FCRA § 1681i(a)(1)(A) deadline.

**Mitigation**

**Metro 2 compliance pipeline**: Build the credit bureau data furnishing pipeline to generate Metro 2 format files using a validated library (e.g., a custom implementation against the Credit Reporting Resource Guide specifications). Automate the following checks on every record before inclusion: judgment date is not in the future, account balance is non-negative, consumer identity fields pass the minimum confidence threshold, and the case status is in the reportable enumeration.

**Dispute intake and investigation workflow**: Provide tenants a verified dispute submission portal. Upon receipt, the platform must: (1) record the dispute with a timestamp, (2) within 5 business days notify all bureaus to which the disputed information was furnished using an automated E-OSCAR dispute response system, (3) investigate the dispute by querying the county court case management system for current case status within 30 days, (4) update or delete the tradeline based on investigation findings, and (5) notify the tenant of the investigation outcome with the updated reporting status. All dispute workflow steps are logged immutably.

**Judgment expiry enforcement**: Eviction judgments are reportable for 7 years from the judgment date under § 1681c(a)(2). A scheduled job identifies and suppresses all tradelines with `judgment_date < NOW() - INTERVAL '7 years'` from the next furnishing cycle. The suppression is logged and the consumer is notified.

**Recovery**

Upon confirmed misreported eviction record, immediately submit a deletion request to all affected bureaus via E-OSCAR (AUD transaction type for deletions). Provide the tenant a written confirmation of the deletion request within 5 business days. If the erroneous record contributed to a housing denial, document the timeline and cooperate with any HUD or CFPB investigation. Conduct a full audit of all furnished records for the affected landlord property to identify any additional misreported items. If systematic furnisher policy failures are identified, engage a CFPB-registered compliance consultant and consider voluntary self-disclosure to the CFPB under their supervisory process, which typically results in lower penalties than enforcement-initiated actions.

## SSN and PII Storage and Transmission Security

### AES-256 Encryption at Rest, TLS 1.3 in Transit, Field-Level Encryption, and HSM-Based Key Management

**Failure Mode**

Social Security Numbers collected during tenant screening or tax form processing (W-9 for landlord payments, 1099 reporting) are stored in plaintext in the `tenant_pii` or `tax_profiles` table. A SQL injection vulnerability, a misconfigured database backup exported to an unencrypted S3 bucket, or a compromised database credential results in bulk exfiltration of SSNs. In a separate failure mode, the application transmits SSN values in plaintext over an internal API call using HTTP between microservices in a VPC, assuming network-layer security is sufficient — but a compromised service or internal traffic analysis captures the values in transit. In a third failure mode, TLS is configured with TLS 1.0 or 1.1 on the external API gateway, exposing communications to POODLE or BEAST attacks.

**Impact**

SSN exposure triggers breach notification obligations in all 50 states (most state laws specifically enumerate SSN as a triggering data element). The FTC Act Section 5 requires reasonable data security; plaintext SSN storage is per se unreasonable per FTC enforcement precedent (*Wyndham, LabMD, Equifax*). IRS Revenue Procedure 98-25 requires reasonable security for electronically stored tax records containing SSNs. Identity theft resulting from SSN exposure causes long-term, difficult-to-remediate harm to affected individuals. A breach of 10,000 SSNs triggers mandatory notification to state AGs in most states and to the IRS if tax records are involved.

**Detection**

**Continuous encryption posture monitoring**:
1. Run a nightly SQL query across all non-encrypted table columns containing column names matching `ssn`, `social_security`, `sin`, `tax_id`, `ein` and verify that stored values match the expected ciphertext format (base64-encoded, minimum 44 characters for AES-256-GCM output). Any plaintext-pattern match (9-digit numeric, with or without hyphens) generates a Critical security alert.
2. Deploy a network traffic analyzer (e.g., Zeek/Bro or AWS VPC Traffic Mirroring with a Suricata IDS) on internal VPC traffic to detect unencrypted SSN patterns in HTTP payloads between services. Alert on any regex match for `\b\d{3}-\d{2}-\d{4}\b` in cleartext HTTP traffic.
3. TLS configuration scanning: Run `testssl.sh` against all external endpoints on a weekly schedule from an external scanning agent. Alert if TLS 1.0 or 1.1 is accepted, if any CBC-mode cipher suites are negotiated, or if the certificate chain includes SHA-1 signed certificates.
4. S3 bucket policy audit: A daily Lambda function enumerates all S3 buckets using the AWS Config rule `S3_BUCKET_SERVER_SIDE_ENCRYPTION_ENABLED` and `S3_BUCKET_PUBLIC_READ_PROHIBITED`. Any bucket storing database backups without SSE-KMS enabled or with public access creates a Critical finding.

**Mitigation**

**Field-level encryption for SSN**:

Implement application-layer encryption of SSN values before they are written to the database. The encryption routine uses AES-256-GCM with a unique 96-bit IV per record, producing an authenticated ciphertext that includes a GCM authentication tag (128-bit). The stored format is: `base64(iv || ciphertext || auth_tag)`. This is distinct from transparent database encryption (TDE) — even if TDE is enabled (e.g., AWS RDS with AES-256 storage encryption), field-level encryption ensures that a compromised database credential cannot yield plaintext SSNs without access to the encryption key.

```
stored_ssn = base64_encode(
  AES_256_GCM_ENCRYPT(
    plaintext_ssn,
    data_encryption_key,
    random_iv_96bit()
  )
)
```

**HSM-based key management**:

Data Encryption Keys (DEKs) used for SSN field encryption are themselves encrypted using a Key Encryption Key (KEK) stored in AWS CloudHSM or HashiCorp Vault with a FIPS 140-2 Level 3 HSM backend. This is the envelope encryption model:
- The DEK is a 256-bit AES key generated per tenant cohort (monthly rotation) and stored encrypted in a `key_store` table: `(key_id, encrypted_dek, created_at, rotated_at, status)`.
- The KEK never leaves the HSM. All DEK wrap/unwrap operations are performed inside the HSM boundary via PKCS#11 or AWS KMS API calls.
- DEK rotation: every 90 days (or immediately upon suspected compromise), a rotation job decrypts all SSN ciphertexts using the old DEK, re-encrypts with a new DEK, and atomically updates both the ciphertext column and the `key_id` foreign key reference in a single transaction. Old DEKs are retired (status = `RETIRED`) and retained for 30 days for decryption of any in-flight records, then cryptographically destroyed (the HSM is instructed to delete the KEK-encrypted DEK blob).

**TLS 1.3 enforcement**:

Configure all external load balancers (AWS ALB, nginx, Cloudflare) with a minimum TLS policy of TLS 1.2 (for compatibility) with TLS 1.3 preferred, and a cipher suite restricted to: `TLS_AES_256_GCM_SHA384`, `TLS_CHACHA20_POLY1305_SHA256` (TLS 1.3 suites), and `ECDHE-RSA-AES256-GCM-SHA384` (TLS 1.2 fallback). Disable all TLS 1.0 and 1.1 cipher suites, all RC4 suites, all export-grade suites, and all anonymous DH suites. Enable HSTS with `max-age=31536000; includeSubDomains; preload` on all external domains. Configure internal service mesh (e.g., AWS App Mesh with Envoy, or Istio) to enforce mutual TLS (mTLS) between all microservices, so that SSN values in internal API calls are encrypted in transit even within the VPC.

**Recovery**

Upon confirmed SSN exposure: (1) immediately rotate the compromised DEK and re-encrypt all SSN ciphertexts using a new DEK — this renders any exfiltrated ciphertexts undecryptable if the KEK was not also compromised; (2) if the plaintext SSNs were exfiltrated directly, engage a breach coach (attorney under privilege to direct forensic investigation), activate the breach response retainer (pre-contracted with a firm like Mandiant or CrowdStrike), and begin state-by-state notification obligation analysis; (3) notify affected individuals within the statutory window (ranging from 30 days in Florida to 72 hours in GDPR jurisdictions) with a letter including the nature of the breach, the data exposed, steps the individual can take (credit freeze, fraud alerts), and one year of complimentary credit monitoring; (4) notify the IRS if SSNs on file for tax reporting purposes were exposed (IRS Form 14039-B for businesses); (5) perform a full key management audit to confirm the KEK was not accessible to the attacker and document the key custody chain for the forensic report.
